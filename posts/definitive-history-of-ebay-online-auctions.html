The Definitive History of eBay: How a Broken Laser Pointer Built a $30 Billion Empire

On September 3, 1995, a 28-year-old software engineer named Pierre Omidyar sat down at his home computer in San Jose, California, and wrote a few hundred lines of code. He called it AuctionWeb — a bare-bones website where people could list items and bid on them. The first item ever sold? A broken laser pointer, for $14.83. When Omidyar emailed the buyer to make sure he understood the thing was broken, the response was legendary: "I'm a collector of broken laser pointers." That single transaction — weird, wonderful, and completely unpredictable — captured the spirit of everything eBay would become.

What happened next is one of the most remarkable growth stories in the history of the internet. AuctionWeb went from a hobby project running on a $30-a-month web hosting account to a publicly traded company worth billions in just three years. It didn't just create online auctions — it proved that strangers on the internet could trust each other enough to exchange real money for real goods. And in doing so, it fundamentally rewired how humans buy and sell things.

The Origin Story Nobody Expected: How eBay Actually Started

Here's the thing most people get wrong about eBay's history: the famous Pez dispenser story is completely fake. For years, the company's official origin myth claimed that Pierre Omidyar built AuctionWeb so his fiancée, Pam Wesley, could trade Pez candy dispensers with other collectors. It was a charming story — a guy builds a website for love. The press ate it up. There was just one problem: it never happened.

The Pez story was invented in 1997 by Mary Lou Song, eBay's first public relations manager, who needed a compelling narrative to pitch to journalists. As Adam Cohen revealed in his 2002 book The Perfect Store: Inside eBay, Song fabricated the entire thing because she thought the real origin story — a programmer wanted to experiment with efficient market dynamics — was too boring for mainstream media. She was probably right about the boring part, but the deception stuck for years before eBay finally acknowledged it wasn't true.

The real story is arguably more interesting. Omidyar, born in Paris to Iranian parents, had moved to the United States as a child and studied computer science at Tufts University. By his late twenties, he was working at a mobile communications startup called General Magic while running a side consulting firm called Echo Bay Technology Group. When he tried to register echobay.com as a domain name, it was already taken — so he shortened it to ebay.com. That's how one of the most recognizable brand names in history was born: from a failed domain registration.

Omidyar's actual motivation for building AuctionWeb was philosophical. He was fascinated by the idea of a "perfect market" — a place where buyers and sellers could find each other directly, without middlemen setting artificial prices. He wanted to see if the internet could create something economists had theorized about for centuries: a marketplace where the true market value of any object would emerge organically through bidding.

From Zero to $1,000 a Month: The First Year of AuctionWeb

AuctionWeb launched on Labor Day weekend 1995. The site was crude by any standard — just text on a gray background, no images, no fancy design. Omidyar hosted it on his personal website, which also included a page about the Ebola virus (one of his other interests at the time) and a list of links he found interesting. The entire operation ran on a single server through his home internet connection with Best Internet Communications, a local ISP that charged him $30 a month.

For the first few weeks, almost nobody came. Omidyar listed a few items himself and posted about the site on Usenet newsgroups — the Reddit of the mid-1990s. Slowly, word spread. People started listing things: old computers, vintage collectibles, random household items. The early community was small but enthusiastic, a collection of bargain hunters, collectors, and curious early adopters who saw something magical in the concept.

Within the first month, AuctionWeb generated about $1,000 in revenue from listing fees — more than enough to cover hosting costs. By the second month, it was pulling in $2,500. Omidyar wasn't charging commissions yet, just small fees to list items. But even that modest income was enough to prove the concept worked. People were actually willing to send money to strangers based on an online listing and a winning bid.

There's a detail here that perfectly captures how scrappy the early days were. Omidyar originally made listing on AuctionWeb completely free. But when his ISP noticed the traffic spike and upgraded his account from a $30 personal plan to a $250 business plan, he started charging listing fees to cover the difference. It was pure necessity, not a business plan. And yet those fees became the foundation of eBay's entire revenue model.

1996–1997: Beanie Babies, Jeff Skoll, and the Pivot to Real Business

By early 1996, AuctionWeb was growing faster than Omidyar could handle alone. He hired Jeffrey Skoll — a Stanford MBA from Canada — as the company's first president. Skoll brought business discipline to what had been a one-man coding project. Together, they incorporated the company in May 1996 and started thinking about AuctionWeb as a real business rather than a hobby.

The timing was perfect, because something bizarre was about to supercharge their growth: Beanie Babies. In 1997, the Ty Inc. plush toys became one of the most insane consumer crazes in American history. People were buying, selling, and speculating on stuffed animals like they were stock certificates. And where did they go to trade them? AuctionWeb. By the end of 1997, Beanie Babies accounted for roughly 10% of all listings on the platform — a staggering number considering the site had hundreds of thousands of categories.

The Beanie Baby boom did something crucial for eBay: it brought mainstream consumers to online auctions for the first time. These weren't tech-savvy early adopters or vintage collectors. They were moms, hobbyists, and casual shoppers who had never bought anything online before. And once they experienced the thrill of bidding — the anticipation, the competition, the rush of winning — they stuck around and started buying other things too.

In September 1997, AuctionWeb officially rebranded as eBay. Revenue for the year hit $5.7 million — not bad for a company that had started as a side project two years earlier. The site had hosted 250,000 auctions and was adding users at a rate that would make most startups weep with envy. But Omidyar and Skoll knew they needed professional management to take things to the next level.

Meg Whitman and the Rocket Ship IPO of 1998

In March 1998, eBay hired Meg Whitman as president and CEO. At the time, the company had exactly 30 employees, about 500,000 registered users, and $4.7 million in annual revenue. Whitman, a Harvard MBA who had previously worked at Hasbro, Disney, and Procter & Gamble, was initially skeptical about joining a quirky internet auction site. But after visiting eBay's modest offices and seeing the passionate community, she took the leap.

It turned out to be one of the best career decisions in Silicon Valley history. Whitman's corporate experience brought structure, marketing savvy, and Wall Street credibility to a company that desperately needed all three. She professionalized the operation while being careful not to destroy the community-driven culture that made eBay special.

Six months after Whitman joined, eBay went public. On September 21, 1998, the company held its IPO on NASDAQ, led by CFO Gary Bengier. The offering price was $18 per share. By the end of the first day of trading, shares had rocketed to $53 — a 194% gain that made it one of the hottest IPOs of the decade. The IPO raised over $60 million, and overnight, Omidyar became a billionaire on paper.

The numbers from 1998 tell the story of a company in hypergrowth. From December 31, 1997 to December 31, 1998, registered users exploded from approximately 340,000 to over 2.1 million — a 6x increase in twelve months. Net revenues hit $47.4 million for the year, a jaw-dropping 724% increase over 1997's $5.7 million. By October 1998, over 700,000 items were listed on eBay across more than 1,000 categories. The marketplace was becoming a genuine economic ecosystem.

How eBay Solved the Trust Problem That Everyone Said Was Impossible

In the mid-1990s, the conventional wisdom was clear: people would never send money to strangers on the internet. The whole concept seemed insane. You're going to wire $200 to someone you've never met, based on a blurry photo and a three-sentence description, and just... hope they mail you the thing? Every business analyst, every traditional retailer, every skeptic said the same thing: online person-to-person commerce couldn't work because there was no way to establish trust.

eBay proved them wrong with a deceptively simple innovation: the feedback system. Introduced in 1997, it allowed buyers and sellers to rate each other after every transaction. Positive ratings accumulated into a reputation score displayed next to every username. A seller with 500 positive reviews and a 99.8% satisfaction rate was, by any reasonable measure, more trustworthy than a random store you'd never visited.

The feedback system was revolutionary because it created what economists call a "reputation economy." Your eBay reputation was an asset — something you'd invested hundreds of transactions building, and something you'd lose if you cheated even once. This made fraud economically irrational for established sellers. Why scam someone out of $50 when you'd lose a reputation worth thousands of dollars in future business?

It wasn't perfect. There were scammers, of course. There were disputes, and the early resolution process was clunky at best. But the system worked well enough to enable something genuinely unprecedented: millions of strangers conducting billions of dollars in transactions with a fraud rate that was, remarkably, lower than traditional retail. eBay didn't eliminate trust issues — it engineered around them.

eBay's Greatest Acquisition: The $1.5 Billion PayPal Deal

By the early 2000s, eBay had a payment problem. Most transactions still relied on personal checks, money orders, or direct bank transfers — all slow, clunky, and decidedly un-internet. Enter PayPal, a scrappy startup founded in 1998 by a group that included Peter Thiel, Max Levchin, and a young entrepreneur named Elon Musk (through the merger with his company X.com).

PayPal had essentially grown up on eBay's platform. By 2002, over 70% of all eBay auctions accepted PayPal as a payment option, and roughly one in four completed auctions used it. PayPal had become eBay's de facto payment system without eBay having any control over it. This was both a testament to PayPal's product-market fit and a strategic vulnerability for eBay.

On October 3, 2002, eBay acquired PayPal for $1.5 billion in stock. At the time, some analysts thought eBay overpaid. In retrospect, it was one of the greatest acquisitions in tech history. PayPal didn't just solve eBay's payment problem — it became a massive standalone business that would eventually grow larger than eBay itself. When eBay spun off PayPal as an independent company in 2015, PayPal's market capitalization quickly surpassed eBay's. As of the mid-2020s, PayPal processes over $1.3 trillion in annual payment volume and is worth roughly three times what eBay is.

The irony is that eBay's best business decision — acquiring PayPal — ultimately benefited PayPal more than eBay. The spin-off freed PayPal to partner with eBay's competitors and expand into markets eBay could never reach. It's like raising a child who grows up to be more successful than you: you're proud, but also maybe a little jealous.

The Auction Model vs. Amazon: Why eBay's Dominance Didn't Last

In 1999 and 2000, eBay and Amazon were roughly the same size. Both were darlings of the dot-com era, both were redefining online commerce, and both had passionate user bases. But they had fundamentally different visions of what e-commerce should look like.

eBay bet on the auction model — the idea that the market should set prices through competitive bidding. It was democratic, exciting, and philosophically aligned with Omidyar's original vision of a perfect marketplace. Amazon, under Jeff Bezos, bet on convenience — fixed prices, fast shipping, and an ever-expanding selection of new goods. Bezos wanted to make buying things online as frictionless as possible.

For years, it looked like eBay's model might win. Auctions were addictive. The thrill of bidding created emotional engagement that fixed-price shopping simply couldn't match. eBay's gross merchandise volume grew from $745 million in 1999 to $14.9 billion by 2003. The company was printing money with minimal inventory risk, since it never actually owned or shipped any products.

But Amazon's bet on convenience proved more durable. As internet usage expanded beyond early adopters, the mainstream consumer didn't want to wait seven days for an auction to end. They didn't want to evaluate seller reputations and hope their item arrived as described. They wanted to click "Buy Now," get free shipping, and receive their purchase in two days. eBay eventually added a "Buy It Now" fixed-price option, but by then Amazon had already claimed the convenience crown.

By the mid-2010s, the divergence was stark. Amazon had grown into a $1 trillion behemoth, while eBay stabilized around $30 billion in market cap. eBay remained profitable and relevant — it still processes over $70 billion in gross merchandise volume annually — but it never recaptured the cultural centrality it had in its first decade.

Then vs Now: Online Auctions in 1998 vs 2026

If you used eBay in 1998, the experience was almost comically primitive compared to today. Listings were text-heavy with maybe one tiny, low-resolution photo that took 30 seconds to load on a 56k modem. There were no buyer protection programs, no "Buy It Now" option, no mobile apps. You bid on something, waited days for the auction to end, mailed a personal check to a stranger, waited for it to clear, and then waited another week or two for your item to arrive in the mail. The entire process could take three to four weeks from bid to delivery.

Payment was the biggest friction point. Before PayPal became dominant, eBay transactions typically involved mailing a money order or personal check. Some sellers accepted credit cards through clunky merchant accounts, but most didn't. The lack of instant payment meant that the average time between winning an auction and the seller shipping the item was often 7-10 business days — just for the payment to process.

Today, eBay processes payments instantly through its own Managed Payments system. Listings have dozens of high-resolution photos, detailed item specifics, and often 360-degree views. The mobile app accounts for over 60% of transactions. Buyer protection guarantees refunds for items not received or not as described. And with options like eBay Guaranteed Delivery, some items arrive within three business days.

The cultural experience has changed too. In 1998, winning an eBay auction felt like an event. You'd refresh the page obsessively in the final minutes, heart racing as the countdown ticked toward zero. People developed strategies for "sniping" — placing a last-second bid to win without driving up the price. There were whole communities dedicated to auction tactics. Today, most eBay purchases are fixed-price transactions that feel identical to buying from any other online store. The auction magic that defined eBay's identity has largely faded.

eBay's Weird and Wonderful Auction Hall of Fame

Part of what made early eBay magical was the sheer absurdity of what people would buy and sell. The platform became famous for bizarre listings that captured the internet's attention and generated free publicity worth millions. A few highlights from eBay's golden age of weird auctions:

In 2004, a woman from Florida listed a grilled cheese sandwich that she claimed bore the image of the Virgin Mary. She'd kept it in a plastic box for ten years, and it had never grown mold (make of that what you will). It sold for $28,000 to an online casino called GoldenPalace.com, which made a career out of buying weird eBay items for publicity.

In 2000, a man tried to sell his soul on eBay. Bidding reached $400 before eBay pulled the listing, citing their policy against selling items that "don't exist." The theological implications of that policy decision have yet to be fully explored.

A town in California — Bridgeville, population 25 — was sold on eBay in 2002 for $1.77 million. It was the first town ever sold online. The buyer backed out, and it was relisted and sold again in 2006 for $1.25 million. Someone got a discount on an entire town.

These stories weren't just amusing — they were marketing gold. Every viral eBay auction reinforced the platform's core value proposition: you never know what you'll find. That sense of discovery and unpredictability was eBay's superpower in the early internet era, and it's something no algorithm-driven shopping experience has ever fully replicated.

The Legacy: What eBay Really Changed About the World

eBay's lasting impact goes far beyond online auctions. It proved several things that the business world had considered impossible in the mid-1990s. First, that strangers on the internet could be trusted to conduct commerce — a foundational insight that enabled everything from Airbnb to Uber to Etsy. Second, that user-generated marketplaces could scale to billions of dollars with minimal overhead — a model that inspired an entire generation of platform businesses. Third, that reputation systems could substitute for institutional trust — an idea that now underpins everything from Yelp reviews to Uber driver ratings.

Pierre Omidyar stepped back from day-to-day operations early, channeling his billions into philanthropy through the Omidyar Network. Jeff Skoll became a Hollywood producer, financing socially conscious films through Participant Media. Meg Whitman went on to run Hewlett-Packard and later became CEO of Quibi (a less successful venture, to put it mildly). The people eBay created went on to shape industries far beyond e-commerce.

Today, eBay remains a $25+ billion company with over 130 million active buyers worldwide. It's no longer the cultural phenomenon it was in 1999, but it still moves more than $70 billion in merchandise annually. The auction model that made it famous now accounts for less than 20% of sales — most transactions are fixed-price. But the core idea that Omidyar had in 1995, sitting at his home computer in San Jose — that the internet could connect buyers and sellers in ways never before possible — turned out to be one of the most consequential insights of the digital age.

And it all started with a broken laser pointer and a guy who collected them.

The entire process could take three to four weeks from bid to delivery.

Payment was the biggest friction point. Before PayPal became dominant, eBay transactions typically involved mailing a money order or personal check. Some sellers accepted credit cards through clunky merchant accounts, but most didn't. The lack of instant payment meant that the average time between winning an auction and the seller shipping the item was often 7-10 business days — just for the payment to process.

Today, eBay processes payments instantly through its own Managed Payments system. Listings have dozens of high-resolution photos, detailed item specifics, and often 360-degree views. The mobile app accounts for over 60% of transactions. Buyer protection guarantees refunds for items not received or not as described. And with options like eBay Guaranteed Delivery, some items arrive within three business days.

The cultural experience has changed too. In 1998, winning an eBay auction felt like an event. You'd refresh the page obsessively in the final minutes, heart racing as the countdown ticked toward zero. People developed strategies for "sniping" — placing a last-second bid to win without driving up the price. There were whole communities dedicated to auction tactics. Today, most eBay purchases are fixed-price transactions that feel identical to buying from any other online store. The auction magic that defined eBay's identity has largely faded.

eBay's Weird and Wonderful Auction Hall of Fame

Part of what made early eBay magical was the sheer absurdity of what people would buy and sell. The platform became famous for bizarre listings that captured the internet's attention and generated free publicity worth millions. A few highlights from eBay's golden age of weird auctions:

In 2004, a woman from Florida listed a grilled cheese sandwich that she claimed bore the image of the Virgin Mary. She'd kept it in a plastic box for ten years, and it had never grown mold (make of that what you will). It sold for $28,000 to an online casino called GoldenPalace.com, which made a career out of buying weird eBay items for publicity.

In 2000, a man tried to sell his soul on eBay. Bidding reached $400 before eBay pulled the listing, citing their policy against selling items that "don't exist." The theological implications of that policy decision have yet to be fully explored.

A town in California — Bridgeville, population 25 — was sold on eBay in 2002 for $1.77 million. It was the first town ever sold online. The buyer backed out, and it was relisted and sold again in 2006 for $1.25 million. Someone got a discount on an entire town.

These stories weren't just amusing — they were marketing gold. Every viral eBay auction reinforced the platform's core value proposition: you never know what you'll find. That sense of discovery and unpredictability was eBay's superpower in the early internet era, and it's something no algorithm-driven shopping experience has ever fully replicated.

The Legacy: What eBay Really Changed About the World

eBay's lasting impact goes far beyond online auctions. It proved several things that the business world had considered impossible in the mid-1990s. First, that strangers on the internet could be trusted to conduct commerce — a foundational insight that enabled everything from Airbnb to Uber to Etsy. Second, that user-generated marketplaces could scale to billions of dollars with minimal overhead — a model that inspired an entire generation of platform businesses. Third, that reputation systems could substitute for institutional trust — an idea that now underpins everything from Yelp reviews to Uber driver ratings.

Pierre Omidyar stepped back from day-to-day operations early, channeling his billions into philanthropy through the Omidyar Network. Jeff Skoll became a Hollywood producer, financing socially conscious films through Participant Media. Meg Whitman went on to run Hewlett-Packard and later became CEO of Quibi (a less successful venture, to put it mildly). The people eBay created went on to shape industries far beyond e-commerce.

Today, eBay remains a $25+ billion company with over 130 million active buyers worldwide. It's no longer the cultural phenomenon it was in 1999, but it still moves more than $70 billion in merchandise annually. The auction model that made it famous now accounts for less than 20% of sales — most transactions are fixed-price. But the core idea that Omidyar had in 1995, sitting at his home computer in San Jose — that the internet could connect buyers and sellers in ways never before possible — turned out to be one of the most consequential insights of the digital age.

And it all started with a broken laser pointer and a guy who collected them.

When was eBay founded and who created it?

eBay was founded on September 3, 1995, by Pierre Omidyar, a French-born Iranian-American software engineer living in San Jose, California. It was originally called AuctionWeb and started as a hobby project hosted on Omidyar's personal website. The name "eBay" came from Omidyar's consulting firm, Echo Bay Technology Group — he wanted echobay.com but it was taken, so he shortened it to ebay.com. The company was officially incorporated in May 1996.

What was the first item ever sold on eBay?

The first item sold on eBay (then called AuctionWeb) was a broken laser pointer, which sold for $14.83 in September 1995. When founder Pierre Omidyar contacted the winning bidder to make sure he understood the laser pointer was broken, the buyer replied that he was "a collector of broken laser pointers." The often-repeated story that eBay was created so Omidyar's fiancée could trade Pez candy dispensers was actually fabricated by a PR manager in 1997 to make the origin story more media-friendly.

How did eBay grow so fast in the late 1990s?

eBay's explosive growth was driven by several factors: the Beanie Baby craze of 1997 (which accounted for 10% of all listings), the introduction of the feedback reputation system that solved trust issues, word-of-mouth from an enthusiastic early community, and the general excitement of the dot-com era. The company went from 340,000 registered users at the end of 1997 to over 2.1 million by the end of 1998 — a 6x increase. Revenue grew 724% in 1998 alone, reaching $47.4 million.

Why did eBay buy PayPal and what happened?

eBay acquired PayPal on October 3, 2002, for $1.5 billion in stock because PayPal had become the dominant payment method on eBay's platform — over 70% of auctions accepted it, and one in four completed transactions used it. eBay needed to control its payment infrastructure. The acquisition proved to be one of the best deals in tech history. In 2015, eBay spun off PayPal as an independent company, and PayPal's market value quickly surpassed eBay's own, growing into a payments giant processing over $1.3 trillion annually.
